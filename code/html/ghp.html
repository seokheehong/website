Project: Armour

When attending the Governor’s Honors Program (GHP55) as a software engineer, an opportunity arose to work on a group final project, where I brought up the idea of an adolescent rehabilitation project, “Project: Armour.” With the purpose of developing more friendly and motivating processes for adolescent physical therapy, this project involves a polyester sleeve with electromyographic (EMG) electrodes that connects to a MyoWare Muscle Sensor. The sensor then sends its data to an online game interface (coded with JS) using an ESP8266, a mini WiFi-microchip with full microcontroller capability like an Arduino. The data is logged separately for medical purposes, while the game is a corresponding simulation of the user’s motions of their arms.

With a limitation of time (just less than two weeks), teamwork was crucial for Project: Armour. Not to mention, the project was far from easy to complete. With frustrations of failure in programming came determined concentration full of extra hours of debugging, and with optimism and encouragement came miraculous progress in our project. Working with my enthused group members led to the thrilling sense of success, concluding Project: Armour with my own intensified passion to create new technological solutions in different scenarios.

After Project: Armour concluded, I took it upon myself to begin the journey of researching efficient electrodes for transradial 3D-prosthetics as my AP Research project (from AP Capstone program). With a future goal of creating affordable yet reliable prosthetics for everyone, this project has come a long and far journey – that continues today.
